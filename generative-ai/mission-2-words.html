Mission 2 — Key Terms

The data terms, frameworks, and concepts you need to understand Mission 2 — and every AI program you lead. Plain language. No jargon. No assumptions.

When a term is still unclear: Ask your AI Buddy — "Explain [term] with an example from my industry." That is what it is there for.
The Data Layer
Data Moat
Core Concept
Your organization's unique data advantage. Anyone can buy the same AI tools. Nobody else has your company's data — your customer feedback, your internal docs, your historical records, your operational data. When you organize that data and make it AI-ready, you build a moat nobody else can cross. The AI Officer's job is to find it, clean it, and make it the foundation of every AI program.
The Three-Layer Sandwich
Framework
The three layers that make up any AI system. Layer one is the user interface — the chat box, buttons, and upload tools. Layer two is the LLM — the brain that processes patterns and predicts outputs. Layer three is the data — what you feed it right now. Most people obsess over layers one and two. The AI Officer obsesses over layer three, because that is where results are made or broken.
Training Data
Technical
The massive dataset an LLM was built on before you ever opened it. Billions of pages of text, code, and research. You did not choose it, you cannot change it, and nothing you do updates it. The model knows what it knows from this layer — and it is fixed.
Application Memory
Technical
The layer where tools like ChatGPT and Claude remember your past conversations, preferences, and saved files. This is the app knowing you, not the AI knowing you. If you switch to a different app running the same underlying model, it has no idea who you are. The memory belongs to the application, not the model.
The Data You Feed It Right Now
Core Concept
The third and most controllable layer of data. What you paste into the prompt, what you upload, and what context you provide in the session. This is the layer that determines whether you get useful output or garbage. The model knows how to think. The app remembers who you are. But what you feed it right now is what determines the result.
Data Quality
Data Checklist
Framework
The four properties that make data AI-ready. (1) Consistent — same format, labels, and structure throughout. "New York" and "NY" and "new york" are three different things to AI. (2) Complete — no missing fields where data should exist. Gaps create blind spots AI will not flag. (3) Labeled — every column, category, and field has a clear name. If a human cannot tell what it means, AI definitely cannot. (4) Connected — related data is linked so the story makes sense. All four, or it is not ready.
Hallucination
Risk
When AI produces incorrect information and presents it confidently. AI does not flag its own errors. It will give you a polished, professional answer built entirely on wrong data if you let it. This is why the AI Officer verifies numbers, audits math, and surfaces assumptions before anything goes to leadership. Always tell AI: "Do not make up numbers."
Garbage In, Garbage Out
Core Concept
The foundational rule of AI data. Feed AI messy, incomplete, inconsistent data and it will produce a confident, professional, completely wrong output. The model does not know the data is bad. It just works with what you gave it. Fixing the data before building the program is the difference between an AI Officer and someone who is just using AI.
Safety & Governance
Free Tier
Risk
The free version of AI tools like ChatGPT or Gemini. When you use the free version for work, your data is very likely being used to train the model. Whatever you paste in — client names, project details, financial numbers — could influence future outputs for other users. Never use free AI tools for work.
Paid Tier
Governance
A subscription-level AI account. Most paid tools stop using your data for training. This is the minimum level for professional work. Your data still goes through their servers and may be stored, but it is not used to train the model.
Enterprise Tier
Governance
An account negotiated between your organization's IT department and the AI provider. Includes contracts about how your data is handled, where it is stored, and what happens to it. The right tier for sensitive company data — salary information, client lists, financial records, legal documents.
Data Governance
Governance
The organizational decisions about which AI tools are approved, what data is safe to use with each tool, and what tier your team should be on. The AI Officer does not just use tools. The AI Officer sets the standard for the team. Data governance is one of the first things you establish when leading an AI program.
Tool Selection
The Four Tool Selection Criteria
Framework
The AI Officer's framework for choosing the right AI tool for a program: (1) The data behind the model — what does it know best? (2) The user interface features — what does your team actually need? (3) The safety controls — what data agreements exist? (4) The apps it integrates with — what is already in your stack? Never start with the tool. Start with what the program needs.
Context Window
Technical
The amount of information an AI model can hold and process in one session. Larger context windows let you upload longer documents, paste more data, and maintain more complex conversations without the model losing track of earlier information. Important when selecting tools for programs that involve large reports or datasets.
