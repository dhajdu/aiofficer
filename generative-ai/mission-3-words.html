🔤 Words to Know

Mission 3 — Key Terms

Frameworks, techniques, and concepts you need to produce professional-grade AI output.

When a term is still unclear: Ask your AI Buddy — "Explain [term] in plain language with an example from [your role or industry]." That's what it's there for.
Prompt Frameworks
RACE Framework
Framework
Your foundation for any structured AI task from Mission 1. In Mission 3, RACE becomes the first pass in a two-pass system: Role (who is AI acting as), Action (what should it do), Context (what is the situation), Example (what does good look like). RACE alone gets you 70% of the way to a usable output.
CRA Framework
Framework
The quality control layer stacked on top of a RACE draft. Three components: Constraints (what are the limits - budget, time, brand rules, scope), Requirements (what must be included - the non-negotiables), Acceptance Criteria (how do you know it is good enough - specific pass-fail checks). RACE gets you 70% of the way. RACE + CRA gets you something your boss can use.
Framework Stacking
Technique
Using two or more frameworks together in sequence to produce better output. In Mission 3, you stack RACE (for the first draft) and CRA (to tighten it). The concept applies throughout the AI Officer program: each framework does a specific job, and combining them in the right order compounds the result.
AI Interview
Technique
The highest-leverage thing in Mission 3. Instead of writing a structured prompt yourself, you tell AI your goal and ask it to ask you questions before starting. AI surfaces gaps in your thinking you did not know were there. The output stops being generic and starts being yours. The AI Interview is not just a prompting technique—it is also the discipline of scoping an AI program before building it.
On-Demand Framework Learning
Technique
The habit of asking AI to teach you the right framework for the problem in front of you, then applying it immediately. You do not need to memorize hundreds of business frameworks. You need the habit of asking AI: "What framework should I use for this?" then "Explain it simply, then apply it to my situation." This skill compounds with every session.
Brand Positioning Statement
Framework
A framework for defining a product's position in the market. Template: "For [target audience] who [need or opportunity], [brand name] is the [category] that [key benefit] because [reason to believe]." In Mission 3, AI teaches you this framework on demand and applies it to BOLT.
Evaluation & Quality
VRC Framework
Quality Control
Your evaluation discipline before any output goes to your manager, a client, or a leadership meeting. Three components: Value (where does the benefit come from - is the payoff clear and specific), Risk (what are the top two ways this fails or misleads), Checkpoint (what is the earliest real-world signal that this output is actually working). VRC scales from a single prompt output to an entire AI initiative.
Acceptance Criteria
Standard
The specific pass-fail standards that define whether output is good enough to use. Part of the CRA framework. Example: "Dana should be able to read any single competitor entry and immediately know what BOLT does differently." Acceptance criteria shift AI from producing generic output to meeting your actual standard.
Professional-Grade Output
Standard
Output that has your name on it and deserves to. The standard in Mission 3 is: could you hand this to your manager, a client, or a leadership meeting right now? Not "is it good enough?" but "would you be comfortable with your name on it?" That standard is what separates prompt users from AI program leaders.
VRC at Scale
Quality Control
Using the VRC framework to evaluate an entire AI initiative—not just a single prompt output. When you are leading an AI program, VRC becomes the discipline you apply before a program gets budget, headcount, or a launch date. Value: where does the ROI come from? Risk: what are the top ways this program fails? Checkpoint: what is the early signal it is working before you are too far in?
Output & Deliverables
Go-to-Market Brief
Deliverable
A structured document that captures everything needed to take a product to market. In Mission 3, the go-to-market brief for BOLT has seven sections: executive summary, market opportunity, competitor analysis, customer profiles, brand positioning, risks, and next steps. This is the final project for Mission 3 certification.
Context Window
Technical
The amount of information an AI model can hold and process in one session. In Mission 3, managing your context well means starting a fresh thread for each major task, uploading your cleaned BOLT dataset at the start so AI has accurate data, and not letting unrelated conversation pollute the context.
Two-Pass System
Technique
The workflow of RACE (first pass) and CRA (second pass) stacked together. First pass gets the content. Second pass locks the quality to the standard you need. Two passes, not two separate processes. When you define what good looks like before you look at the output, you will know if AI got there.
